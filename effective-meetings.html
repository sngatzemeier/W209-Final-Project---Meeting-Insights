Effective Meetings

Organizations and team members spend significant time in preparing and conducting meetings. Such meetings can be seen as a critical tool for increasing collaboration, innovation, and communication across the organization. In Harvard Business Review study, it was indicated that managers can spend over 90% of their time in meetings driving decisions. It is critical to know if the meetings being conducted are effective and efficient. This is even more critical in the virtual meeting environment.

Are your meetings effective and efficient?

Effectiveness of meetings can be measured using many metrics including agenda, decisions made, meeting follow-up, to name a few. One of the critical measures is meeting engagement of attendees. Engagement can be impacted and measured by many factors. In our product analysis, we measure engagement of attendees using a few variables.

  • How many meetings are happening measured by organizer function, level, and timeline?
  • What are some of the meeting statistics in terms of invited attendee behavior?
  • What is the duration of the meetings by organizer function, level, and timeline?
  • How many emails are being sent during the meetings by meeting duration and organizer?
  • Are attendees multitasking during the meetings?
  • Do attendees with meeting conflicts multitask more during meetings?

Right Meeting Audience

Meetings can be inefficient and ineffective with too few or too many attendees. [CEO Today]. Do you have right attendees and appropriate acceptance?

How long should meetings last? Getting attendees to join a meeting is the first step. Can you keep them engaged [Nook]? So how long should a meeting last?

Number of Meetings

This report provides number of meetings over the research time period. The report also provides a movie of the data by day of week. The data is aggregated for all organization types and meeting organizer level. This provides an overview of the data. From this overview, we can conclude that the meeting load is relatively uniform across the weekdays with small variations and on Saturdays there are very few meetings. From this overview level, users can filter the data by organizer function, level in the organization, and day of week to understand more granular details. According to HBR review, 65% of senior managers said meetings keep them from completing their own work. 64% said meetings come at the expense of deep thinking. Can you study if your organization is suffering from similar issue and which functions across the company suffer from similar problems.

Right Meeting Audience

From this overview report, users can understand across the company how many people are invited for meetings? how many invitees suffer from meeting double booking? how many invitees accept and reject the meetings. The data is plotted by organizer function type and also provides the flexibility for users to filter the data further by organizer level. This type of faceting provides sufficient flexibility for the user. From the data, we can conclude that <70% of team members accept meeting invitations and between 25-40% of invitees are double booked. Users can further filter and understand the data. While organizing meetings, organizers need to know following five rules: (1) Know your numbers (2) Be realistic about who will attend (3) Choose the right personalities (4) Do not double up on skills and insights and (5) Cut out the guilt invites. Can you identify which organizer function types and organizer levels are having more success with meeting invitees acceptance levels?

Length of Meetings

Length of meetings can have a significant impact on productivity and meeting efficiency. We humans seem to focus best in 45-minute increments [Link]. Team members tend to be distracted with longer meetings. The overview of dataset provides number of meetings as a function of meeting duration across the organization. It uses color encoding to provide details by organizer function type and organizer level. In addition, we are also providing the opportunity for users to select a team period and facet the data further by organization function type over the period of interest. By reviewing the data can users identify which organizations and organizer levels has more meetings with higher meeting duration. Users can also determine during which time period these long meetings are happening.

Email Distractions During Meetings

This overview report provides average number of emails sent during a specific meeting by organization function and meeting organizer level as a function of meeting duration. We can expect that with increasing meeting duration, the average number of emails sent during the meeting will increase and this indicates distraction and disengaged team members with longer meetings. Users can further understand the data and granular details by faceting to a specific time period of interest of by reviewing the data for a certain meeting organizer function type. Users are also able to play an animation of the data as a function of day of the week. Using this data users can identify which organizer functions are inefficient as measured by meeting distraction (number of emails sent during the meetings). In addition, users can also understand if there is a correlation to meeting organizer level and day of the week when meeting distractions are higher.

Multitasking During Meetings

This last report measures multitasking during meetings. There is no clear definition of multitasking and it can be sending emails during meetings or sending/reading texts during meetings or engaged in a side conversation, to name a few. We do not have clear definition of the quantity of activities that will be defined as multitasking during meetings. The top left report presents percentage of attendees multitasking during a meeting as a function of organization type. This view can be filtered by selecting a particular function type. The chart below this top chart provides the average percentage of attendees with conflicts. We hypothesize that higher the number of attendees with conflicts, a higher percentage of attendees will be multitasking. We also provide a view of average percentage of attendees multitasking as a function of percentage of attendees with conflicts and use size encoding to denote the meeting organizer level. We hypothesize that higher the meeting organizer level, fewer attendees will be multitasking. Using this report users can identify which organization functions and organizer levels are facing a problem with multitasking during the meetings.
